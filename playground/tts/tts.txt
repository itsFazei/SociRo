The Dragon in the Needle 

Mia was ten years old, and she had been in the hospital for two days. 

She didn't mind the bed, or even the food. What she minded was the thing the nurse had mentioned that morning. 

A blood draw. 

She pulled her blanket up to her chin and stared at the ceiling. Her dad was sitting beside her, pretending to read a magazine. 

"You're doing the ceiling-staring thing," he said. "Want to hear a story?" 

"I'm ten," Mia said. "I'm not five." 

"It's about a dragon." 

She considered this. "Fine." 

"Once," he said, "there was a girl called Seren who lived near a mountain where a dragon lived. Everyone whispered about it black scales, burning eyes, a roar that could knock a man flat. 

One day, the king asked Seren to climb the mountain. A silver moss grew near the dragon's cave, the only thing that could cure a sickness spreading through the village. 

'But I'm scared,' Seren said. 

'The bravest people,' the king told her, 'are the ones who are afraid and go anyway.' 

She thought of the sick children. She thought of her little brother's cough. She went. 

Her hands shook the whole way up. She kept thinking about turning back. But she kept walking. 

When she reached the cave, the dragon lowered its great head, and sniffed her. A warm, enormous sniff that ruffled her hair. Its eyes were amber and gold, not red. It looked, she realised, curious. Not angry. Just curious. 

'I need the moss,' Seren said. 'For the sick children.' 

The dragon turned, walked to the far wall, covered in glowing silver-green moss, and sat down, watching her calmly. 

Seren gathered the moss as fast as she could. When she turned to leave, the dragon almost looked pleased. 

She walked back down the mountain faster than she'd come up. Halfway down, she noticed the shaking in her hands had stopped." 

Mia's dad paused. 

"Fear shrinks," he said quietly, "when there are other things in the space alongside it. The dragon's sniff. The moss. Her brother getting better. The fear was still there, but it had less room." 

Mia looked at her arm. "Is it going to hurt?" 

"A little. Like a pinch. But what's the blood draw for?" 

"To check if the medicine is working," she said. 

"Like the moss," her dad said. 

When the nurse came in, she moved slowly, unhurried. The needle was smaller than Mia had imagined. 

"Can I watch?" Mia asked. 

"Whichever feels right," the nurse said. "There's no wrong way." 

Mia decided to watch. She breathed out slowly. There was a quick pinch. sharp for just a second. and then it was over. 

"All done," the nurse said. "You did brilliantly." 

Mia pressed the cotton pad to her arm and felt strange and proud and a little wobbly, all at once. She thought of Seren walking down the mountain, the moss safe in her bag, hands finally still. 

She felt, just a little, like someone who had climbed one too. 

The End.
